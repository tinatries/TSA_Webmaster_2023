BluFord Guion

Our spaceship, the Bluford I, is named after one of the greats of astronomical history: Guion Bluford. Born in Philadelphia in 1942, he went on to get a degree in aerospace engineering from Penn State in 1964. In 1972, he would return to his academics after joining the war efforts in Vietnam, earning a doctorate in the same field as his degree. He became one of the 35 NASA trainees who were selected to go into space, making him the first African American to do so. By the end of his career, he had gone into space 4 times.

Cryogentic Engine

We recognize the pollution caused by rocket launches, therefore, we utilize clean energy – cryogenic fuel. Cryogenic engines use liquid hydrogen(LH2) and liquid oxygen(LOX) as propellants. Cryogenic fuels provide greater thrust and power than other engines due to its high mass flow rate. These engines are actively in use because of their high efficiency, low mass, and low pollution.

Cryogenic engines have been used in multiple space missions, including NASA’s journey to the moon on Saturn V. In our missions, the engine we use will be 2.1 meters tall with a dry weight of 220 kg, producing a thrust of 190 kN. Similar engines include CE 20 and RL 10.

Launch

Before any paying customer is allowed to board, our ships undergo an extensive review process to ensure all systems are working smoothly. Once all customers and crew members are safe and secured in their seats, it’s time for launch. It takes between eight and nine minutes for our ships to reach orbit when launched at about 18,000 miles per hour. From then on you can enjoy incredible views from space.

Recovery

Unfortunately all journeys do have to end and once this journey comes to a close our state-of-the-art spaceship is prepared for a safe touchdown back to Earth. Based on where the landing takes place whether in sea or by land, the process for recovery changes.

For land touchdown is achieved through the rocket boosters firing upwards using reserve propulsion to land along with parachutes with sea recoveries being just parachutes. In both cases after landing, the rocket pod can easily be recovered through a ship or buggy sent to pick up the astronauts after their successful journey.
